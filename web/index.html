The Algorithm Knows You... Or Does It?

Does it feel like you are always watching the same five channels? Do you feel trapped in a feedback loop where the "Rich-Get-Richer" and the famous just get more famous?

YouTube's recommendation algorithm is a "black box" that prioritizes clicks over connection, often hiding the most vibrant communities from view.

But what if we could redraw the map?

We believe that the true geography of YouTube isn't defined by metadata or view counts, but by you — the users.

Rebuilding YouTube from the Bottom Up

To bypass the traditional recommendation system, we mapped YouTube from the bottom up by focusing on real human behavior. We profiled consistent, socially validated users and tracked how they migrate between channels.

By analyzing 8.6 billion comments, we built a community network where channels are connected solely by shared commenters.

Our Research Questions

  • User Behavior: Is commenting structured enough to build a meaningful network?
  • Organic Structure: What structures emerge when we connect channels through people, not algorithms?
  • Recommendation: Can we build a transparent recommendation engine based on human interaction?

Two Ways to Explore the Data

To answer these questions, we didn’t follow a single path. Instead, we discovered that two complementary journeys emerge from the same dataset.

Both journeys begin with the same foundation — 8.6 billion comments and millions of engaged users — but they tell two very different stories about how YouTube actually works.

So… where do you want to start?

  • The Network Journey
    In this path, we guide you. We begin by carefully cleaning and validating billions of comments to isolate genuine human engagement. From there, we connect channels through shared audiences, revealing the large-scale structure of YouTube — its hubs, bridges, and hidden communities — as seen through an analyst’s lens.
  • The User Journey
    In this path, you take over. Instead of starting from predefined labels, you step back and decide how behavior itself should be compared. By grouping users with identical commenting patterns, you explore recurring ways people actually interact with YouTube — discovering structure not because we point it out, but because you observe it emerge.
